Project:

Overview:

Diabetes is a term used to describe a group of conditions that affect the way in which the human body manages the amount of sugar in the blood. People with the condition cannot maintain a healthy level of glucose in their blood, as their bodies do not produce sufficient amounts of insulin – a hormone responsible for the conversion of glucose into energy. As such, their blood sugar levels must be monitored. This project offers a hardware solution for monitoring blood sugar through the use of a non-invasive wristband, backed by software in the form of a mobile app that connects to the monitor, and provides the user with updates relating to insulin intake, blood sugar levels, and food/carb intake.

Motivation:

The prevalence of diabetes throughout the Australian (and worldwide population) is only increasing. Specifically, Type 2 diabetes, a proven consequence of obesity, will continue to increase in commonness as the average life becomes more sedentary and our diets become higher in sugar and caloric content (Diabetes Australia, 2015). Though the effects of diabetes can be extremely harmful, it is also a manageable condition. Through the injection of insulin to lower blood sugar, and the application of a controlled diet to increase blood sugar, people can enjoy a life free from the effects of diabetes. There already exists monitors and apps to help with these processes, but through separate applications. Creating a hardware solution with software integration that not only tracks blood sugar non-invasively, but also tracks food and carb intake should provide a more convenient and reliable way to manage diabetes.

Description:

The implementation of this project has two main components: a blood sugar monitor and a mobile phone app that connect via Bluetooth. These two will work together to get accurate data from the user’s body, and then represent and transform this data in a useful manner. The direct coordination between the monitor and the app should ideally provide a more convenient way of managing diabetes for both the people with the condition, and their medical practitioners.

The hardware monitor:

The monitor ideally would be designed in a similar vein to popular fitness trackers made by companies such as Fitbit or Gamin. A wearable to be worn like a watch, with a smaller profile so that it doesn’t draw attention. The main functionality of the blood sugar monitor is as follows:
1. Read blood-sugar level non-invasively
Current blood sugar monitors require the user to prick their finger to draw blood and wiped on a small strip of paper. This is inserted into the meter, and the blood sugar calculated in millimoles per litre (mmol/L). Based off this measurement, the amount of insulin the person is required to take is calculated using a ratio provided by a medical professional and is reported back to the user. Instead of using blood, the device will have optical sensors that use infrared light to determine the blood sugar level (Laska, 2019), removing the need to draw blood and saving costs by removing one-time use materials.
2. Record blood-sugar level every fifteen minutes
Rather than requiring the person to actively record their blood sugar, the device will record a reading every 15 minutes. This will be done passively and requires no input from the user. Each recording is saved to the accompanying app.
3. Vibrate to notify wearer when glucose levels are too high or too low
The end result of monitoring the blood sugar level of a person is to make them aware when it is reaching unsafe levels, whether that be too high or too low. When the person approaches either extreme, the include vibration motor in the device provides haptic feedback – one long vibration for too high, two short vibrations for too low.
4. 7 Day battery life with fast charging speeds
The device’s battery must be long enough to last a full week and be easily rechargeable through USB-C standard, so as to minimise time off the wrist. Ideally, is should also be easily maintainable or repaired so to make troubleshooting easier.

The Mobile Application:

The accompanying app needs to be designed simply enough to be usable for all ages and be developed for both Android and IOS platforms. The core functions of the app are:
1. Record blood sugar readings from the wearable
Every 15 minutes, the wearable will send a reading to the phone which is recorded in the app. This data is accessible by the user and presented in a graph that shows blood-sugar (mmol/L) over time (hours) for the current day. If the limits of blood sugar are reached, the application calculates how much insulin is required using the pre-set ratio supplied by a medical practitioner, and records how much was taken at the time.
2. Allow the user to record dietary information
An important part of managing diabetes is controlling your diet. The application will allow the user to input the food they have eaten throughout the day and will allow for input of how many grams of carbs are consumed as well. A section to allow the user to leave comments will also be included and the entered information will be timestamped throughout the day.
3. Allow weekly and monthly reports to be printed
The app will also be able to print either a monthly or weekly report, summarising the blood sugar level, amount of insulin taken, and amount of carbs eaten over each day throughout the period (including user comments). This should be helpful to provide to doctors when they need to analyse how the user’s management is proceeding.

Tools, Technologies and Skills:

The biggest focus in this project is the development of optical blood sugar monitors. This will need to be done to a standard that is medically accurate and is small enough to sit in a footprint similar to that of a wristwatch. Experience in developing optical sensors in the medical space will be ideal for this project, as will skill in manufacturing small electronics that are reliable and maintainable. In terms of the application, the software can be written in whatever language is convenient, such as languages like Java for Android, or Swift for IOS devices. A point of importance is the stability of the application, as people will rely on it for medical advice.

Outcome:

The overall goal of this project is to make the management of diabetes more convenient for people that have the condition. By streamlining and creating a more cohesive experience that tracks not just blood sugar levels, but also dietary and insulin intake – and does so through a non-invasive, passive means – the active participance of managing diabetes should be reduced, relieving stress. As diabetes becomes more prevalent, its important to refine the ways we manage it. This project should not only achieve that, but also open the door way in terms of wearable technology that can be made useful in monitoring a person’s everyday health.
